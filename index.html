What is the most underrated social issue of our time? How would you address it?

Improving familial connections for those impacted by incarceration.

When an individual is incarcerated, a sustained family connection becomes expensive, fragile, and hard to repair after conflict. That breakdown does more than just strain relationships during incarceration; it destabilizes caregiving, harms children’s emotional security, and leaves people returning home with fewer stabilizing ties.

This project focuses on incarcerated parents/caregivers and their family members on the outside. Through this proposed product, I hope to support the many children whose parents are incarcerated, reduce recidivism, and make familial connections stronger through reentry, without policing each family's unique culture.

Motivation

Once a week, I facilitate a parenting program inside Cook County Jail at 3015 S. California Blvd. The people I work with speak fondly of their children and consistently express a strong desire to be good parents, all while navigating the constraints of incarceration.

However, over time, I've noticed the same frictions coming up again and again. To name a few - co-parenting conflict that escalates without repair, calls that become the only touchpoint and then break after one argument, distrust of a program language that feels culturally agnostic, and (most structurally) how hard it is to practice any skills consistently when contact with anyone from the outside is scarce.

This motivated me to think about what it takes to design a parenting intervention that actually works under carceral constraints. Where communication and access are limited, emotional bandwidth is low, and stress is ever-prevalent.

The Problem

In most U.S. carceral institutions, family communication is treated like a scarce privilege. It is expensive, vaguely scheduled, and easy to lose after conflict.

Notes from inside jail

In sessions, conversations about parenting quickly become conversations about relationships: conflict with partners, uncertainty about authority at home, feeling excluded from family decisions, and fear that children will grow emotionally distant. Several fathers described arguments that led caregivers to stop answering the phone — sometimes for weeks.

Participants often raise differences in cultural practices and expectations around child-rearing. Even when a curriculum is evidence-based, “good parenting” can be communicated in a way that feels normative — meaning it assumes one culturally specific “right” way to parent. This often causes the 28/35 black participants to undermine the curriculum.

But the biggest constraint is structural: relationships are built through repetition, and inside jail sustained contact is hard. So even when people learn something useful, it’s difficult to practice it enough for it to stick.

Takeaway: when connection is fragile, the family system destabilizes — and reentry starts from a weaker base.

Research on relationships and recidivism

Research on recidivism consistently finds that people who maintain strong family connections during incarceration are less likely to return to prison (Bales & Mears, 2008; Mears et al., 2012). Visitation, phone contact, and other sustained communication are associated with improved post-release outcomes, suggesting that relationships function as a stabilizing force during reentry.

Relationships are built through repetition. When contact is restricted, it sets off downstream effects that show up in families long after incarceration ends.

Takeaway: healthy, consistent connection is the basis for stability and reentry.

Solution: a relational credit economy

The model is simple: providing sustained, voluntary engagement that unlocks more ways to stay connected

How engagement is designed

Engagement formats are built for constrained environments: short calls, limited emotional bandwidth, and asynchronous communication. They target specific frictions that repeatedly show up in incarceration-affected families: repair after conflict, co-parenting alignment, emotional regulation, and maintaining routines with kids.

Development is co-designed with practitioners (family therapists, reentry staff, program facilitators) and people with lived experience, so the tools don’t assume one “right” cultural model of parenting.

What credits unlock

Credits unlock expanded connection options — for example, more flexibility in timing and format (minutes, messaging windows, video time), and lower-friction ways to stay in touch.

Rewards expand access; they do not require disclosure of relationship content. The logic stays transparent and predictable.

Feasibility

This does not necessarily require new hardware. Many facilities already use secure tablets for messaging, account access, and approved content. The primary build is the application layer: credit logic, activity library, and a simple interface that works under real constraints.

Ethics & reasoning

What this approach does

  • expands opportunity for connection
  • lets families define what meaningful engagement means to them
  • supports practicing skills under real constraints
  • keeps the logic transparent

What it avoids

  • imposing “one right way” parenting norms that can reduce trust
  • using engagement data for punishment
  • policing relationship content or dynamics

Guardrails

  • No content surveillance: the system does not read or score calls, messages, or relationship content.
  • Data minimization: it stores only what’s needed to compute credits and unlocks (e.g., activity completion + totals).
  • Co-design: an advisory group with lived experience reviews the activity library and reward structure.
  • Opt-in pathways: families can choose from multiple engagement formats and skip anything that feels irrelevant.
  • Transparency + appeal: clear rules for unlocks and a process to resolve errors or disputes.

By “normative parenting models,” I mean interventions that assume a culturally specific “right” way to parent — which can alienate families and reduce participation.

Beyond incarceration: many families struggle with fragmented connection, conflict, distance, and financial strain. Building systems that support repair and continuity is a broader social need — but incarceration makes the stakes and constraints impossible to ignore.

That’s why I think family connection during incarceration is one of the most underrated social issues of our time: it sits upstream of child wellbeing, caregiver stability, and reentry success.
