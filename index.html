What is the most underrated social issue of our time? How would you address it?

Designing systems that sustain family connection during incarceration

Mass incarceration is intertwined with some of the United States’ most persistent social challenges - racial inequality, residential segregation, housing instability, unequal access to education and healthcare, and gun violence. Yet policy conversations often treat crime as an individual outcome rather than a structural one. When people are removed from their families and communities, the consequences extend far beyond prison walls - relationships are strained, economic mobility is disrupted, and the conditions that produced vulnerability to crime in the first place are often reinforced. Without interventions that support family stability, this cycle persists.

Motivation

Once a week, I facilitate a parenting program inside Cook County Jail at 3015 S. California Blvd. The individuals I work with consistently express a strong desire to be present for their children, even while navigating the constraints of incarceration. Over time, however, I began to notice recurring gaps between the program’s intentions and its practical impact. This prompted a broader question — what would it take to design interventions that function more effectively under carceral constraints?

The Problem

In Jail, communication with family is a scarce resource and privilege — expensive, time-limited, and fragile.

Notes from inside Jail

During sessions, broad conversations about parenting and caregiving often drift into lived experience — conflict with partners, questions of authority in the family, feeling excluded from family decisions, loss of control, domestic fights that lead to children being cut off, alongside the fear that kids will move on without them.

Both cohorts are predominantly Black (14/15), and participants often raise differences in cultural practices and expectations around child-rearing. Although our curriculum is evidence-based, it has pushed me to think more critically about what “normative” good parenting practices are.

Yet the biggest constraint is structural: inside jail, sustained contact is hard, so skills rarely get practiced enough to stick.

Research on the Efficacy of Parenting Programs

Troy et al. (2018) note that while prison-based parenting programs are often viewed as meaningful and can improve parents’ confidence and skills, the evidence on long-term effectiveness is limited and mixed, with few studies including sustained follow-up to assess whether initial benefits persist over time.

If relationships are built through repetition, limiting contact limits relational change.

Scale

This isn’t niche. It’s one of those problems we treat like “someone else’s issue,” but it touches millions of kids and reshapes family life in ways we don’t measure well.

What makes this underrated: it looks like an “individual failure” (bad parents, bad choices), but a lot of it is the predictable outcome of a system that makes ordinary repair and continuity hard.

Solution: a relational credit economy

Instead of framing communication as a limited resource to control, this approach treats it as stabilizing infrastructure. Access expands through sustained relational effort, without policing the content of relationships or forcing participation.

Earn credits through engagement

  • guided conversation prompts (short-call friendly)
  • co-parenting exercises
  • shared journaling (asynchronous)
  • family goal-setting
  • structured check-ins
  • reflection modules
  • memory match game
  • more games

Designed for constrained settings: low bandwidth, short windows, and asynchronous options.

Unlock expanded connection

  • additional call minutes
  • extended messaging access
  • video visitation privileges
  • added minutes to 'good time'

Tiered + auditable. Voluntary participation. Families define what “meaningful engagement” means.

Feasibility

This does not necessarily require new hardware. Many facilities already use secure tablets for messaging, account access, and approved content. Primary work will go into building the application.

Ethics & Reasoning

What this approach does

  • expands opportunity for connection
  • lets families define meaningful engagement
  • supports practice under constraints
  • keeps logic auditable and transparent

What it avoids

  • imposing normative parenting models
  • using engagement data for punishment
  • policing familial-relationship dynamics

Guardrails

Apart from time spent and points cumulated, no data will be collected. The objective is to make sustained connection more feasible and avoid monitoring participants.

Beyond incarceration: A lot of families aren’t incarceration-affected, but still struggle with thin, fragmented connection, conflict, distance, money, and time. Building systems that support repair and continuity is a general design need.
