What is the most underrated social issue of our time? How would you address it?

Designing systems that sustain family connection during incarceration

One of the most underrated social issues in the United States is mass incarceration, particularly its racial disparities. We often focus on crime as individual behavior, but pay less attention to what happens to the families and communities people are removed from. Incarceration strains relationships, makes reentry more difficult, and increases the likelihood of returning to prison — creating a cycle that is both socially harmful and costly. We spend billions on incarceration, yet far less on the stability that helps people successfully come home. Supporting families should therefore be seen as part of the solution: when family connections remain strong, individuals are better positioned to reintegrate, which ultimately supports public safety and reduces long-term public spending (Bales & Mears, 2008; Mears et al., 2012; Arditti, 2012).

Motivation: Once a week, I facilitate a parenting program inside Cook County Jail at 3015 S. California Blvd. The individuals I work with are very motivated to become better parents despite significant constraints. Yet, over the past four months, I’ve begun to notice recurring breakdowns in the program’s real-world effectiveness. Consequently, I started thinking about how we could increase impact.

Problem

Correctional systems often describe family connection as predictive of safer reentry, but operationally, communication is treated like a scarce privilege — expensive, time-limited, and fragile.

What I see inside

Sessions rarely stay only about “parenting tips.” They drift into the real stuff: conflict with partners, authority, feeling excluded from family decisions, and the fear that kids will move on without them.

But the biggest constraint isn’t lack of care. It’s that sustained contact is structurally hard — so the skills never get repeated enough to stick.

Design tension

  • We ask people to strengthen relationships…
  • …while structuring the environment to make sustained connection difficult.
  • The challenge is not purely behavioral. It’s organizational.

One sentence that matters: If relationships are built through repetition, limiting contact limits relational change.

Scale

This isn’t niche. It’s one of those problems we treat like “someone else’s issue,” but it touches millions of kids and reshapes family life in ways we don’t measure well.

What makes this underrated: it looks like an “individual failure” (bad parents, bad choices), but a lot of it is the predictable outcome of a system that makes ordinary repair and continuity hard.

Solution: a relational credit economy

Instead of framing communication as a limited resource to control, this approach treats it as stabilizing infrastructure. Access expands through sustained relational effort, without policing the content of relationships or forcing participation.

Earn credits through engagement

  • guided conversation prompts (short-call friendly)
  • co-parenting exercises
  • shared journaling (asynchronous)
  • family goal-setting
  • structured check-ins
  • reflection modules
  • memory match game
  • more games

Designed for constrained settings: low bandwidth, short windows, and asynchronous options.

Unlock expanded connection

  • additional call minutes
  • extended messaging access
  • video visitation privileges
  • added minutes to 'good time'

Tiered + auditable. Voluntary participation. Families define what “meaningful engagement” means.

Feasibility

This does not necessarily require new hardware. Many facilities already use secure tablets for messaging, account access, and approved content. Primary work will go into building the application.

Ethics & Reasoning

What this approach does

  • expands opportunity for connection
  • lets families define meaningful engagement
  • supports practice under constraints
  • keeps logic auditable and transparent

What it avoids

  • imposing normative parenting models
  • using engagement data for punishment
  • policing familial-relationship dynamics

Guardrails

Apart from time spent and points cumulated, no data will be collected. The objective is to make sustained connection more feasible and avoid monitoring participants.

Beyond incarceration: A lot of families aren’t incarceration-affected, but still struggle with thin, fragmented connection, conflict, distance, money, and time. Building systems that support repair and continuity is a general design need.
