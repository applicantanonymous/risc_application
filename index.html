What is the most underrated social issue of our time? How would you address it?

Designing systems that sustain family connection during incarceration

Mass incarceration is intertwined with some of the United States’ most persistent social challenges - racial inequality, residential segregation, housing instability, unequal access to education and healthcare, and gun violence. Yet policy conversations often treat crime as an individual outcome rather than a structural one. When people are removed from their families and communities, the consequences extend far beyond prison walls - relationships are strained, economic mobility is disrupted, and the conditions that produced vulnerability to crime in the first place are often reinforced. Without interventions that support family stability, this cycle persists.

Motivation

Once a week, I facilitate a parenting program inside Cook County Jail at 3015 S. California Blvd. The individuals I work with consistently express a strong desire to be present for their children, even while navigating the constraints of incarceration. Over time, however, I began to notice recurring gaps between the program’s intentions and its practical impact. This prompted a broader question — what would it take to design interventions that function more effectively under carceral constraints?

The Problem

In carceral institutions in the U.S., communication with family is a scarce resource and a rare privilege — expensive and fragile.

Notes from inside Jail

During sessions, broad conversations about parenting and caregiving drift into lived experience — conflict with partners, uncertainty about authority at home, feeling excluded from family decisions, and the fear that children will grow up emotionally distant.Several fathers spoke about arguments that led caregivers to stop answering the phone, sometimes for weeks.

Both cohorts are predominantly Black (14/15), and participants often raise differences in cultural practices and expectations around child-rearing. Although our curriculum is evidence-based, it has pushed me to think more critically about what “normative” good parenting practices are.

Yet the biggest constraint is structural: inside jail, sustained contact is hard, so learned skills rarely get practiced enough to stick.

Research on Relationships and Recidivism

Research on recidivism consistently finds that individuals who maintain strong family connections during incarceration are significantly less likely to return to prison (Bales & Mears, 2008; Mears et al., 2012). Visitation, phone contact, and other forms of sustained communication are associated with improved post-release outcomes, suggesting that relationships function as a stabilizing force during reentry.

If relationships are built through repetition, then restricting contact does more than separate families — it quietly undermines one of the strongest protective factors against recidivism.

My Solution: A System for Sustained Connection

Participants earn relational credits through structured engagement designed for constrained environments, accounting for short calls, limited emotional bandwidth, and asynchronous communication.

Earn points through engagement

  • guided conversation prompts
  • co-parenting exercises
  • shared journaling
  • family goal-setting
  • structured check-ins
  • reflection modules
  • memory match game
  • more games

Unlock expanded connection

  • Additional call minutes
  • Extended messaging access
  • Increased video visitation time
  • Added minutes to 'good time'

The system is transparent. It does not monitor or evaluate activity or relationship content. Families define what meaningful engagement looks like for them.

Feasibility

This does not necessarily require new hardware. Many carceral facilities already use secure tablets for messaging, account access, and approved content. Primary work will go into building the application.

Ethics & Reasoning

What this approach does

  • expands opportunity for connection
  • lets families define what meaningful engagement means to them
  • supports practicing skills learned under constraints
  • keeps logic transparent

What it avoids

  • imposing normative parenting models
  • using engagement data for punishment
  • policing familial-relationship dynamics

Guardrails

Apart from time spent doing activities and points cumulated, no data will be collected.

Beyond incarceration: A lot of families aren’t incarceration-affected, but still struggle with fragmented connection, conflict, distance, and financial strain. Building systems that support repair and continuity is a larger social need that has the potential to support families beyond this context.
